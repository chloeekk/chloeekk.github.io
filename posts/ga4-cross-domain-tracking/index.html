Cross-domain tracking in Google Analytics 4

In this tutorial, I’ll demonstrate how to set up cross-domain tracking in Google Analytics 4. We’ll begin by discussing the issue that cross-domain tracking addresses, followed by a step-by-step guide to the configuration process.

What is cross-domain tracking in Google Analytics 4?

Cross-domain tracking in Google Analytics 4 is a technique enabling webmasters, marketers, and analysts to transfer user or visitor identifiers from one domain (Domain A) to another (Domain B), while maintaining session information. This approach ensures that the visitor is tracked consistently as the same individual across different websites or domains.

For this to function effectively, both websites involved must utilize the same GA4 data stream, meaning they should share the same measurement ID.

How does cross-domain tracking work in Google Analytics 4?

When a visitor moves from domainA.com to domainB.com, Google Analytics 4 appends a unique parameter to the URL of domainB.com. This parameter enables the Google Analytics instance on domainB.com to inform Google Analytics 4 that it is, in fact, the same individual browsing across two different websites.

Will Google Analytics track subdomains?

Correct, and for this purpose, cross-domain tracking is not necessary.

When tracking various websites within the same domain, such as blog.yourwebsite.com and www.yourwebsite.com, Google Analytics 4 automatically manages this.

Therefore, if you’re wondering whether a subdomain is treated as a separate domain in this context, the answer is NO. Tracking across subdomains within the same primary domain doesn’t require cross-domain tracking in Google Analytics 4.

Steps to configure cross-domain tracking in Google Analytics 4

Before proceeding, bear in mind a crucial prerequisite: both websites you intend to incorporate into cross-domain tracking must be using the SAME Google Analytics 4 (GA4) property. This code, whether directly integrated into the site (gtag) or implemented via Google Tag Manager (GTM), must be under the same property.

Step #1: Navigate to the ‘Admin’ section in Google Analytics

Select ‘Data Streams’, and then choose the relevant web data stream

Step #2. Go to Configure Tag Settings

Step #3. Click Configure your domains

Step #4: Input all the domains that you want to include in your cross-domain tracking configuration

Once you’ve added them, click ‘Save’ to apply the changes.

For this process, there’s no need to set up a referral exclusion list, as GA4 automatically manages this aspect. However, if you happen to notice your own domains appearing as traffic sources, you have the option to adjust the settings for unwanted referrals.

Important to note: Making changes here will also impact the automatic tracking of outbound clicks in Enhanced Measurement. Domains listed will no longer be considered as sources of outbound clicks.

Step #5: Test the setup

After saving your modifications, wait for about a minute and then visit your first website (for example, domainA.com). On this site, locate a link that directs visitors to domainB.com.

Click on this link. When you are redirected to the second domain, observe the URL. It should include the _gl= parameter followed by a string of characters.

Note: Consider the Role of Redirects

If the URL on domainB.com lacks the _gl parameter, a JavaScript redirect could be interfering. To diagnose this, use the Redirect Path extension.

First, install the extension.

Then, visit domainA.com and follow the link to domainB.com. Click on the Redirect Path extension to check if the _gl parameter was initially present but subsequently lost. If this is the case, consult with your developers for further debugging. The objective is to ensure the persistence of the _gl parameter throughout the redirect process.
