In the capital markets, a major IPO is not only a financial milestone for corporate fundraising, but also a high-intensity integrated marketing campaign centered on brand equity and market confidence. When a recovering macro environment meets a high-quality target with strong certainty, the ability to systematically convert “corporate value” into “subscription momentum” becomes a core indicator of marketing professionalism.

This article will explain what a comprehensive IPO integrated marketing plan should include.

Evaluating the Target’s Investment Value — Why Is This IPO Worth Promoting?

Before allocating significant resources to large-scale marketing, you first need to clarify the target’s underlying logic. Not every IPO has inherent appeal. The marketer’s job is to uncover the hard, persuasive reasons that can convince investors.

You can assess and shape the narrative from the following three dimensions:

1. Macro Environment Assessment: Is the Current Timing Attractive to the Market?

The success of an IPO depends heavily on market sentiment cycles. You need to determine whether the current external environment can support the offering.

  • Market index performance: Observe whether the broader market index is near recent highs or in an upward trend. An active market environment can significantly reduce the difficulty of acquisition. When the market is strong, investors are usually more willing to enter.
  • Recent IPO performance: Review the first-day price performance of newly listed stocks over the past 1–3 months. If most recent IPOs have performed well after listing, that becomes excellent marketing material. If performance has been mediocre, then you will need to identify other differentiated selling points.

2. Background Strength Assessment: Can You Leverage “Group Endorsement”?

This is an important dimension for evaluating the target’s stability. Marketers need to clarify the relationship between the target company and its parent company (if any).

  • Transfer of parent company credibility: If the IPO target is a subsidiary of a large group, you should evaluate the parent company’s historical performance in the capital markets. The parent company’s brand awareness, the stock price performance of its listed business segments, and its historical dividend track record are all trust assets that can be directly leveraged.
  • Business synergy value: Examine whether the parent company and its subsidiary have complementary businesses. Map out the parent company’s business presence in other sectors. A group with deep roots across multiple industries—such as construction, education, and retail—naturally gives its subsidiary’s IPO stronger perceived resilience. For example, a group with a large portfolio of shopping malls, hospitals, and hotels automatically gives its subsidiary’s IPO access to a highly aware pool of potential audiences.
  • Note: If the target does not have this kind of group background, the focus should shift to the founding team, core technology, or market share.

3. Core Competitiveness Assessment: Does the Target Have a “Leading Player Effect”?

Investors naturally gravitate toward representative companies.

  • Offering size: Assess the IPO’s fundraising size and valuation. Is it the “largest in the market in recent years” or the “first in the sector”? Labels like these can automatically attract media attention and institutional subscriptions.
  • Industry profile: Does the target operate in a defensive industry, such as healthcare or utilities? During periods of economic volatility, these sectors are especially attractive to upper-middle-income and high-net-worth investors seeking stable returns.
  • Shareholder incentives: Check whether there are incentive policies for existing shareholders, such as bonus share distributions or priority subscription rights. These types of policies can directly boost engagement among existing users.

To make the case for “target value assessment” more convincing, marketers cannot rely on qualitative descriptions alone. They must cite verifiable hard data. Below are the core data points and suggested sources marketers should collect for the three dimensions discussed above:

When writing integrated marketing content, data should not simply be listed out. It should function as an “evidence chain” that supports the underlying logic:

  1. Build trust through index constituent status

If the parent company of the target is a member of a core index (such as the top 30 stocks in the Malaysian market), marketers should emphasize: “This target has already met institutional-grade screening standards.” Inclusion in a core index means it has already passed the exchange’s strictest requirements in terms of market capitalization, liquidity, and compliance.

  1. Convert recognition through ETF composition

If the parent company is a heavyweight holding in multiple major ETFs, this can serve as a form of “passive endorsement.” Marketers can use this to tell audiences: even if you are not yet familiar with this subsidiary, leading global asset managers such as BlackRock or Vanguard have already expressed recognition of its business ecosystem by holding shares in its parent company.

  1. Quantify scarcity through industry comparisons

Do not simply say “it is a big company.” Use data from the prospectus for comparison. For example:

“According to industry data disclosed in the prospectus, the target’s number of hospital beds / market share is X times that of the second-largest player in the sector, while its IPO pricing P/E is Y% below the industry average.”

Consolidating Company Details — Building an IPO Communications Asset Library

After completing the underlying assessment of the target’s investment value, the marketer’s focus should shift to “information consolidation.” The goal at this stage is to transform dry financial and compliance data into a multi-dimensional marketing asset library that can communicate with the market and resonate with audiences.

1. Official and Authoritative Channels: The Foundation for Message Consistency

All external communications in a marketing campaign must be based on official disclosures. This is the prerequisite for avoiding regulatory risk and establishing professionalism.

  • Prospectus (Prospectus / DRHP): Marketers should focus on extracting the “use of proceeds.” If the funds will be used for expansion—such as adding hospital beds or developing new technologies—this becomes part of the “growth story.” If the funds will be used to repay debt, the messaging should positively frame it as “optimizing capital structure.”
  • Investor Relations (IR) page: Check whether the company website has a standalone IR section. A high-quality IR page usually includes historical performance charts, management profiles, and ESG (Environmental, Social, and Governance) reports. These non-financial indicators are often crucial for attracting institutional investors and high-quality retail clients.

2. Core Deal Information: Quantifying Subscription Value

These data points directly determine investors’ willingness to act and are also the main source for designing promotional materials:

  • Key dates: Clarify the prospectus launch date, subscription closing date, allotment date, and final listing date.
  • Offering parameters: Including the offering price, total number of shares issued, and the estimated post-listing market capitalization.
  • Benchmark metrics: Collect P/E comparison data between the target and listed peers in the same sector. If the target is priced below the industry average, this becomes a highly persuasive “low-barrier entry” selling point.

3. Social Media and Public Sentiment: Capturing Market Emotion

Understanding what potential investors are discussing is more important than simply pushing ads.

  • Sentiment monitoring: Search company-related keywords on Facebook, Instagram, or local financial forums (such as Malaysia’s Stock123).
    • Positive sentiment: Are investors expecting dividend payouts? Do they have natural goodwill toward the parent company’s brand?
    • Negative concerns: Does the market think the valuation is too high? Or are there misconceptions about the industry’s outlook?
  • Demand insight: Based on this feedback, marketers should create an FAQ to anticipate and resolve user concerns before the formal campaign begins.

4. Content Ecosystem Analysis: KOLs and Media Direction

External opinions are an important reference point influencing retail investor decisions.

  • Analyst and KOL views: Look for commentary on the IPO from independent financial analysts or finance content creators. Record their core arguments as material for “third-party endorsement” in marketing copy.
  • Media coverage frequency: Track how often mainstream financial media (such as The Edge or Bloomberg) report on the deal. Dense media exposure can significantly strengthen the project’s credibility, and marketers should consolidate these article links into the promotional landing page.

At this stage, it is recommended to build an “information index sheet” that maps all of the above data points to their exact sources—for example, page X of the prospectus or the parent company announcement link. This not only improves content production efficiency, but also ensures that every marketing claim is traceable during cross-departmental review or compliance checks.

Target Audience Analysis — Finding the Most Precise Conversion Entry Points

Once target value assessment and information consolidation are completed, the marketing plan enters its most critical stage: target audience analysis.

Marketers must recognize that IPO subscription is not a “one-size-fits-all product.” Different audience groups have completely different levels of risk tolerance, capital lock-up sensitivity, and brand familiarity. Therefore, you need to break audiences into segments and design differentiated communication strategies for each.

1. Existing Brand Audiences: High-Net-Worth and Highly Loyal Groups

These audiences are typically deep users of the parent company’s or group’s existing businesses—for example, frequent customers of its healthcare institutions, owners of its properties, or premium members of its malls.

  • Profile: Upper-middle-income or affluent individuals, generally older and more mature. They have strong trust in the parent company’s brand and hold a certain amount of idle capital.
  • Core pain point: Their existing asset allocation channels are relatively limited, often concentrated in fixed deposits or real estate. In the current market environment, they want exposure to market upside, but remain cautious about complex financial derivatives.
  • Hook: “Certainty and an extension of brand value.” Emphasize that this IPO is an opportunity to share in the long-term growth dividends of the group. Since they are already users of the brand, marketers should use this sense of familiarity to lower the decision-making threshold.

2. Digital Retail Investors: A Group Seeking Efficiency and Leverage

These audiences are typically active users of digital brokerage platforms.

  • Profile: Investors with some market experience, familiar with online operations, and sensitive to market volatility. They care more about transaction costs and acquisition efficiency.
  • Core pain points:
    1. Liquidity pressure: Large IPOs usually involve a relatively long capital lock-up period.
    2. Low allotment rates: Popular IPOs are often heavily oversubscribed, and small capital commitments may not receive ideal allocations.
  • Hook: “Capital leverage and zero-cost trial.” Highlight the platform’s IPO financing tools, emphasizing benefits such as “low interest / no interest if not allotted” and “zero subscription fees.” Show them how digital tools can help them pursue a higher allotment probability at a lower effective cost.

3. Incremental Growth Segments: Cross-Ethnic / Cross-Regional New Audiences

In multicultural markets such as Malaysia, this often refers to groups that have not yet been deeply converted, such as high-income Malay audiences.

  • Profile: They have basic awareness of the brand, but may not yet hold accounts on a specific brokerage platform. They have investment potential, but may still have blind spots regarding securities investment compliance or the operational process.
  • Core pain point: “Entry barriers and operational obstacles.” They may perceive stock investing as too complicated, or may be unsure whether the investment complies with their specific financial principles (for example, whether it is Shariah-compliant).
  • Hook: “Simplified, compliant education.” Use multilingual content to emphasize the simplicity of the platform experience and the target’s authoritative compliance credentials. Build initial awareness through “scenario-based marketing,” such as brand exposure in places they frequently visit.

To make audience analysis more data-driven, marketers are advised to pull data from the following sources:

  • Internal platform data:

    • Analyze the subscriber composition of previous large IPOs of a similar type (such as 99 Speedmart): who were they, and what was the average subscription amount?
    • Review the asset distribution of current users: which users hold a large amount of idle cash but have never subscribed to an IPO?

  • Third-party market research:

    • Refer to reports such as Statista or local bank-issued wealth management reports to understand average disposable income and investment preferences across different ethnic groups and age brackets.

  • Social listening:

    • Use Google Trends or social media analytics tools to observe search interest trends for keywords such as “Sunway” and “IPO” across different language environments.

The logic of target audience analysis is this: do not try to say the same thing to everyone. Talk about “stability and trust” to high-net-worth audiences, “cost and leverage” to retail investors, and “accessibility and convenience” to incremental new audiences.

Key Messages & USP — Distilling the Communication “Hook”

The goal of this section is to turn complex company data and platform tools into a concise hook that immediately lands with the audience. Marketers need to build these core messages from two dimensions: the target itself and the subscription channel.

1. Target Key Message

This part addresses the investor’s core question: “Why should I subscribe to this company?” Based on the target’s objective characteristics, marketers should distill exclusive and differentiated selling points.

  • Positioning through market status: Emphasize the scale and industry influence of the target.

“The largest IPO in this market in the past decade” or “A leading healthcare services provider moving toward Main Market listing.”

  • Communicating certainty: Use the group background to build psychological confidence.

“Backed by 50 years of industrial foundation from XX Group, offering exposure to the long-term growth dividends of its core assets.”

  • Guiding return expectations: Clearly explain any relevant allocation or shareholder benefit policy.

“Secure early access to high-quality assets through IPO subscription, with attention to its long-term stable dividend potential.”

2. Platform USP

In IPO marketing, the target is the “product,” while the subscription platform is the “venue.” Marketers need to highlight the efficiency advantages of digital brokerages compared with traditional channels in order to drive the final conversion action.

  • Cost efficiency:

    • Core selling point: $0 subscription fee.

    Eliminate investors’ trial-and-error costs, especially when allotment is uncertain, ensuring that users incur no additional administrative fees when placing a subscription.

  • Capital leverage:

    • Core selling point: IPO Financing.

    By offering high-multiple leverage, the platform helps investors increase their subscription amount without tying up too much cash flow, thereby improving their allotment probability.

  • Risk mitigation:

    • Core selling point: “0 interest if not allotted” or “ultra-low financing cost.”

    Provide a targeted offset mechanism for the biggest concern among retail investors: interest loss caused by capital being tied up without receiving an allotment.

Note that all key messages must be grounded in facts from the prospectus. Absolutely avoid misleading, overly absolute wording such as “guaranteed rise” or “sure profit.” Instead, use objective phrasing such as “historical performance” or “policy design.”

Integrated Promotion Strategy

A successful IPO integrated marketing campaign should not end when the user clicks “Subscribe.” It should continue into the second-stage conversion after allotment results are announced.

2. Promotion Timeline: From Pre-heat to Subscription

  • Phase 1: Awareness Pre-heating (Pre-launch Period)

    • Core focus: Begin SEO content deployment and social media seeding. Use “market whispers” and forward-looking industry content to build the initial perception among core audiences that this IPO is worth watching.

  • Phase 2: Existing User Activation and Referral Expansion (Prospectus Launch / Subscription Period)

    • Core focus: Push traffic from all channels to the subscription landing page. Add sharing components to the subscription page, and package transfer-in campaigns around incentive points such as “1 bonus share for every 10 shares.” At the same time, use automated Push notifications and EDMs to fully reach existing high-potential users.

  • Phase 3: Precision Conversion (Subscription Countdown Period)

    • Core focus: Increase ad allocation weight. Licensed relationship managers conduct one-on-one outreach to high-net-worth clients.

3. Anti-Churn Mechanism: Solving the Problem of “Withdraw Funds Immediately If Not Allotted”

This is one of the most overlooked parts of IPO marketing. Marketers must anticipate the psychological drop users may feel after failing to receive an allotment.

  • Scenario 1: Buffering after allotment results are announced

    • Action: At the same time allotment results are released, precisely push an idle cash value-enhancement plan to users who did not receive shares.
    • Logic: Tell users that although this subscription was unsuccessful, keeping their funds on the platform can still generate returns above the benchmark rate, preventing immediate withdrawal.

  • Scenario 2: Guiding users toward a second opportunity (stock performance and upcoming IPO previews)

    • Action: Embed a “Malaysia Stocks Top Gainers List” or “next S-tier IPO preview” on the failed subscription page.
    • Logic: Use ongoing market momentum to shift user attention from “this failed attempt” to “the next opportunity,” thereby retaining funds within the platform.

  • Scenario 3: Automated reactivation of new users

    • Action: For new users who deposited funds because of the IPO campaign, trigger automated communications on D+1, D+3, and D+7 after the allotment date.
    • Logic: Use targeted trading vouchers or wealth management bonus coupons to encourage them to try secondary market trading or other financial products, completing the transition from an “IPO-only user” to an active trading user.

During execution, a data feedback loop is critical. You need to monitor in real time the share rate of the subscription landing page, the conversion costs of different advertising channels, and the post-subscription fund flow ratio. Based on this data, you can continuously adjust ad creative direction and Push notification frequency.
