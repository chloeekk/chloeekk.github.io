What is a cold email?

A cold email refers to an unsolicited email that is sent to a recipient who has no prior relationship with the sender or their company. It is a way of introducing a brand, product, or service to new prospects.

Cold emailing is commonly used as an email marketing technique, but it should not be confused with spam emails. When done properly, cold emails are personalized and relevant to the recipient’s interests. Cold emailing can be an effective strategy for reaching new prospects, generating leads, and closing deals in business-to-business (B2B) sales. It is important to comply with anti-spam laws, such as the CAN-SPAM Act or the GDPR, to ensure legal and ethical practices in cold email campaigns.

What is the difference between cold email and email marketing?

Cold email and email marketing are two different approaches to reaching out to potential customers and nurturing relationships with existing prospects. Here are some key differences between cold email and email marketing:

Purpose and Target Audience

  • Cold Email:

Cold emails are used to initiate contact with new prospects who have no prior relationship with the sender. The goal of cold emailing is to generate leads and start conversations.

  • Email Marketing:

Email marketing focuses on nurturing and maintaining relationships with existing prospects and customers. It aims to provide value, build trust, and encourage engagement.

Relationship with the Sales Process

  • Cold Email:

Cold emails are typically associated with the sales process. They are a direct outreach strategy to generate interest, qualify leads, and move them through the sales funnel.

  • Email Marketing:

Email marketing is often part of the broader marketing process. It involves delivering targeted and personalized content to engage and retain customers, encouraging repeat purchases and brand loyalty.

Personalization and Volume

  • Cold Email:

Cold emails are usually personalized to some extent, addressing the recipient’s specific needs or pain points. However, they are often sent in larger volumes to reach a wider audience.

  • Email Marketing:

Email marketing campaigns can be highly personalized, leveraging customer data and segmentation to deliver tailored content and offers. However, they are typically sent to a more defined and specific target audience.

Metrics and Measurement

  • Cold Email:

Cold email success is often measured by metrics such as response rates, meeting scheduling, and conversions from initial contact to sales opportunities.

  • Email Marketing:

Email marketing success is measured by metrics like open rates, click-through rates, conversion rates, and overall campaign performance. It focuses on long-term engagement and customer lifetime value.

It’s important to note that the effectiveness of both cold email and email marketing depends on various factors, including the quality of the email content, targeting, personalization, and adherence to legal and ethical practices.

Are cold emails considered legal?

Cold emails are not considered illegal if they comply with relevant regulations and laws, such as the CAN-SPAM Act in the United States or the GDPR in the European Union. The legality of cold emails depends on factors like the content, purpose, and compliance with opt-out requirements. Here are some key points:

  • CAN-SPAM Act:

In the United States, cold emails are legal under the CAN-SPAM Act as long as they comply with certain rules. These rules include providing accurate sender information, using truthful subject lines, including a clear opt-out mechanism, and honoring opt-out requests promptly.

  • GDPR:

In the European Union, the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) does not ban cold emailing, but it emphasizes the need for lawful bases and consent when processing personal data. Cold emails must comply with GDPR requirements, such as having a legitimate interest or obtaining explicit consent from individuals.

  • Opt-Out Mechanism:

Both the CAN-SPAM Act and GDPR require cold emails to include a clear and easy-to-use opt-out mechanism. Recipients should have the option to unsubscribe from future emails and their requests should be honored promptly.

  • Personalization and Relevance:

Cold emails should be personalized, relevant, and targeted to specific recipients. Mass-sending generic and unsolicited emails without any relevance to the recipient’s interests or needs can increase the risk of being perceived as spam.

It is important to note that laws and regulations may vary across different countries and regions. It is recommended to consult legal professionals or seek guidance from regulatory authorities to ensure compliance with applicable laws.

Do people open cold emails?

Cold email open rates can vary depending on various factors such as the quality of the email, the subject line, personalization, and the target audience. While there is no definitive answer to whether people open cold emails, here are some statistics and insights that provide an idea of the open rates:

  • Average Cold Email Open Rates:

The average open rate for cold emails in 2023 is reported to be around 23.9%. However, this can vary based on industry, target audience, and email content.

  • Subject Line Impact:

Approximately 33% of people decide to open or not open an email based solely on the subject line. Crafting compelling and engaging subject lines can significantly impact the open rates.

  • Timeliness of Opening:

According to Yesware data, around 75% of all cold emails are opened within one hour of being sent. This highlights the importance of delivering relevant and timely content to increase the chances of getting noticed.

  • Personalization and Response Rates:

Personalized cold emails tend to have higher response rates compared to generic ones. Including the recipient’s name, company, or pain points in the subject line or email copy can significantly increase the chances of a reply. Woodpecker data suggests that personalized cold emails have an average response rate of 17%.

What are some of the benefits of cold emailing?

Cold emailing, when done effectively, can offer several benefits:

  • Reaching users where they spend the most time:

Cold emails allow you to reach potential customers or contacts directly in their inbox, which is a platform they frequently engage with.

  • Persistence:

Cold emails provide a persistent means of communication. Even if the initial email is not opened or responded to, follow-up emails can help maintain visibility and increase the chances of getting a response.

  • Scalability:

Cold emails are scalable, meaning they can be sent to a large number of recipients simultaneously. This makes it an efficient method for reaching a broader audience and generating leads.

  • Networking:

Cold emails can be used as a tool for networking. By reaching out to professionals in your industry or potential business partners, you can establish new connections and expand your network.

  • Lead Generation:

Cold emails can be an effective strategy for lead generation. By targeting specific individuals or businesses that match your ideal customer profile, you can generate leads and potentially convert them into customers.

  • Cost-effective:

Cold emailing can be a cost-effective marketing strategy compared to traditional advertising methods. It eliminates the need for printing materials or paying for ad space, making it a budget-friendly option for outreach.

  • Personalization:

When done well, cold emails can be personalized to resonate with the recipient’s needs and interests. This personal touch can increase the chances of engagement and response rates.

The ideal length of a cold email can vary, but several studies suggest that keeping it concise is key. Here are some insights on the ideal length of a cold email:

  • According to a study by HubSpot, emails between 50 and 125 words had a response rate above 50%.

  • Boomerang’s study found that the best-performing email length for cold emails is between 50 to 125 words, resulting in higher response rates.

  • QuickLines.ai suggests that the ideal length for a cold email is between 50-200 words.

  • SalesWingsApp notes that there is no set length for cold emails, as it depends on your goals and audience. However, guidelines recommend aiming for 50 to 150 words.

  • OctopusCRM recommends a word count of 50 to 150 words, which translates to roughly 5 to 15 lines of text.

  • Lavender.ai’s data suggests that the optimal length for a cold email is between 25 and 50 words.

How to write a cold email?

To write an effective cold email, follow these steps:

  1. Research your target audience Understand who your ideal recipient is and gather information about their role, company, and any relevant details that can help you personalize the email.

  2. Define your objective Clearly define the purpose of your cold email. Is it to introduce your product/service, schedule a meeting, or build a relationship? Having a clear objective will help you structure your email effectively.

  3. Start with a compelling subject line Craft a subject line that grabs attention and entices the recipient to open the email. Use personalization, curiosity, or a benefit-oriented approach to make it engaging.

  4. Personalize the greeting Address the recipient by their name in the greeting. Avoid generic salutations like “Dear Sir/Madam.” Personalization adds a touch of warmth and shows that you’ve done your research.

  5. Introduction Introduce yourself briefly and establish credibility. Mention any common connections, mutual interests, or relevant achievements that can capture the recipient’s interest.

  6. Establish relevance Connect your offer or message to the recipient’s needs or pain points. Show them why your email is relevant to their situation and how it can benefit them.

  7. Provide value Clearly articulate the value proposition of your product, service, or request. Explain how it can solve a problem or provide a solution for the recipient.

  8. Keep it concise and focused Cold emails should be concise and easy to read. Avoid lengthy paragraphs and use bullet points or short sentences to convey your message effectively.

  9. Call to action Clearly state what action you want the recipient to take. Whether it’s scheduling a call, requesting a demo, or asking for feedback, make it clear and easy for them to respond.

  10. Personalize further Customize your email by including specific details related to the recipient’s industry, challenges, or interests. This shows that you’ve tailored the email specifically for them.

  11. Add credibility Include social proof, testimonials, or case studies to build trust and credibility in your email. This can increase the recipient’s confidence in your offer.

  12. Polite closing End your email with a polite closing, such as “Thank you for your time,” “Looking forward to connecting,” or “Best regards.” Sign off professionally.

  13. Follow-up plan Have a well-defined follow-up strategy in place. If you don’t receive a response, consider sending a polite follow-up email after a few days or a week. Persistence can increase your chances of getting a response.

  14. Proofread and test Before sending the email, proofread it for grammar, spelling, and clarity. Test the email on different devices and email clients to ensure it appears correctly.

  15. Track and analyze Use email tracking tools to monitor the open and response rates of your cold emails. Analyze the data to identify patterns and make improvements to your approach.

Remember, each cold email should be tailored to the individual recipient and offer value. Avoid using generic templates and focus on building a genuine connection through personalized communication.

Avoid common mistakes

When writing cold emails, it’s important to avoid common mistakes that can hinder your chances of success. Here are some key mistakes to avoid:

  • Lack of personalization:

Avoid sending generic, impersonal emails. Take the time to research the recipient and tailor your email to their specific needs or interests. Personalization can significantly increase your chances of getting a response.

  • Overly long emails:

Keep your cold emails concise and focused. Long, rambling emails can overwhelm the recipient and make it less likely for them to read the entire message. Stick to the key points and keep the email easy to digest.

  • Focusing on yourself instead of the recipient:

Don’t make your cold email all about you and your company. Instead, focus on how your product or service can benefit the recipient. Highlight the value they will gain by engaging with you.

  • Lack of clarity and structure:

Make sure your cold email has a clear structure and is easy to follow. Use short paragraphs, bullet points, and headings to break up the text and make it scannable. Clearly state your purpose and call to action.

  • Pushy or salesy language:

Avoid using pushy sales language that can turn off the recipient. Instead, adopt a friendly and helpful tone. Focus on building a relationship and initiating a conversation rather than making a hard sell.

  • Spelling and grammar errors:

Proofread your email carefully before sending it. Spelling and grammar mistakes can create a negative impression and undermine your professionalism. Use tools like spell checkers or ask someone to review your email.

  • Neglecting to follow up:

Don’t assume that the recipient will respond to your initial cold email. Follow up politely after a reasonable period of time. A well-timed follow-up can increase your chances of getting a response.

  • Failure to provide a clear call to action:

Make it easy for the recipient to take the desired action. Clearly state what you want them to do, whether it’s scheduling a call, downloading a resource, or providing feedback.

  • Sending mass emails without segmentation:

Avoid sending the same cold email to a large group of recipients. Segment your audience and tailor your emails based on their specific needs and interests. This shows that you’ve done your homework and increases the relevance of your email.

  • Neglecting to test and optimize:

Test different variations of your cold email to see what works best. Track the open and response rates and analyze the data to make improvements to your approach over time.

10 killer cold email templates

Creating effective cold email templates requires a balance of personalization, value proposition, and clear calls-to-action. Here are ten killer cold email templates that you can use as a starting point, but remember to customize them to suit your specific situation and target audience:

Template 1: Introduction and Value Offer

Subject: [Recipient’s Name], How We Can Help [Company/Person]

Hi [Recipient’s Name],

I hope this email finds you well. My name is [Your Name], and I’m reaching out from [Your Company]. We specialize in [briefly describe your product/service], and I noticed that [mention a common challenge or opportunity for the recipient’s industry or role].

I thought our [product/service] could help you [specific benefit or solution]. If you’re interested, I’d love to schedule a quick call to discuss how we can assist you.

Looking forward to your response!

Best regards, [Your Name]

Template 2: Personalized and Complimentary

Subject: Impressed by Your [Recent Achievement/Work]

Hi [Recipient’s Name],

Congratulations on your recent [achievement/work/project]. I came across your [mention where you found their work] and was truly impressed by [specific detail that impressed you].

At [Your Company], we believe that collaboration can lead to incredible results. I’d love to explore potential ways we can work together to achieve even greater success.

Could we schedule a call to discuss this further?

Thanks and regards, [Your Name]

Template 3: Offering Help or Assistance

Subject: [Recipient’s Name], Assisting with [Specific Goal/Challenge]

Hi [Recipient’s Name],

I noticed that you’re working on [mention their goal/challenge], and I believe I can provide some assistance. At [Your Company], we have successfully helped businesses like yours [mention relevant results or case studies].

If you’re open to it, I’d be happy to share some strategies that could help you reach your goals faster.

Let me know your thoughts.

Best regards, [Your Name]

Template 4: Mutual Connection Introduction

Subject: Introduction from [Mutual Connection’s Name]

Hi [Recipient’s Name],

I hope you’re doing well. [Mutual Connection’s Name] recommended that I get in touch with you. They mentioned that you might be interested in [mention a relevant topic/industry].

I’d love to learn more about your work and see if there are any ways we can collaborate or support each other.

Looking forward to connecting soon!

Best regards, [Your Name]

Template 5: Engaging Question

Subject: Quick Question About [Recipient’s Industry/Interest]

Hi [Recipient’s Name],

I have a quick question that I think you’d be the perfect person to answer. How do you see [mention a relevant industry trend/issue] impacting [Recipient’s Company/Industry] in the next six months?

Your insights would be greatly appreciated.

Thanks, [Your Name]

Template 6: Personalized Case Study Approach

Subject: Case Study: How [Your Company] Helped [Similar Company] Achieve [Results]

I came across [Recipient’s Company] and couldn’t help but notice the similarities between your company and [Similar Company]. We recently worked with them to achieve [specific results or achievements], and I thought you might find their case study valuable.

Would you be interested in reviewing their success story?

Best regards, [Your Name]

Template 7: Offering Valuable Resource

Subject: [Recipient’s Name], Valuable Resource for [Their Interest/Goal]

Hi [Recipient’s Name],

I found your interest in [their interest/goal] intriguing. I thought you might appreciate our [e-book/report/toolkit] on [relevant topic]. It provides valuable insights and actionable tips to help you [specific benefit].

You can access it here: [Link to the resource].

Feel free to reach out if you have any questions or need further assistance.

Thanks, [Your Name]

Template 8: Personalized Video Message

Subject: [Recipient’s Name], A Personal Message for You

Hi [Recipient’s Name],

I hope you’re having a great day! I wanted to take a moment to introduce myself and share a quick video message specifically for you. You can view it here: [Link to the video].

I’d love to connect and learn more about your goals and how we might be able to collaborate.

Looking forward to hearing from you!

Best regards, [Your Name]

Template 9: Asking for Advice

Subject: Seeking Advice from [Recipient’s Expertise/Experience]

Hi [Recipient’s Name],

I’m impressed by your expertise in [Recipient’s expertise/industry], and I’d be grateful for your advice. I’m currently working on [briefly describe your project/idea], and I believe your insights could be invaluable.

Would you be open to a short chat to share your thoughts?

Thank you, [Your Name]

Template 10: Invitation to Industry Event/Webinar

Subject: Join Us at [Industry Event/Webinar] - Special Invite for You

I hope this email finds you well. We are hosting an exclusive [Industry Event/Webinar] with some of the brightest minds in the [recipient’s industry/interest].

Best regards, [Your Name]

Remember, the key to effective cold emails is personalization, offering value, and being respectful of the recipient’s time and interests. Customize these templates to suit your unique situation and make genuine connections with your recipients.

How to follow up on a cold email?

Following on a cold email is an important step in building relationships and increasing the chances of getting a response. Here are some tips on how to effectively follow up on a cold email:

  • Personalize your follow-up:

Reference your previous email and personalize the message to show that you remember the recipient and their specific needs or interests. This demonstrates that you value their time and are genuinely interested in connecting with them.

  • Keep it concise and clear:

Keep your follow-up email short and to the point. Highlight the key points from your initial email and remind the recipient of the value or benefits they could gain from your offer or proposition.

  • Add value:

Provide additional information, insights, or resources that can add value to the recipient’s business or interests. This shows that you are invested in their success and not just pushing your own agenda.

  • Use a compelling subject line:

Craft a compelling subject line that grabs the recipient’s attention and encourages them to open your follow-up email. Make it intriguing, relevant, and personalized to increase the chances of getting a response.

  • Offer alternative options:

If you haven’t received a response, provide alternative options for the recipient to engage with you. This could include suggesting a different time for a call or meeting, offering additional channels of communication, or proposing an alternative solution that may better suit their needs.

  • Be polite and respectful:

Maintain a polite and respectful tone throughout your follow-up email. Avoid being pushy or demanding. Instead, express understanding of their busy schedule and reiterate your willingness to assist or address any concerns they may have.

  • Set a reasonable timeline:

Give the recipient a reasonable timeframe to respond. This helps establish a sense of urgency while allowing them enough time to consider your offer or request. Consider including a gentle reminder or deadline in your follow-up email.

  • Follow up more than once:

If you still haven’t received a response after your initial follow-up, it may be appropriate to send a second or even third follow-up email. However, avoid spamming or overwhelming the recipient with too many messages. Space out your follow-ups and adjust your approach each time.

Remember, every situation is unique, and the effectiveness of your follow-up will depend on various factors such as the recipient’s preferences, timing, and relevance of your initial email. Be patient and persistent while maintaining professionalism and respect in your follow-up communications.

Please note that cold emailing practices may vary based on industry, country, and individual preferences. Always comply with applicable laws and regulations regarding email communication and privacy.
